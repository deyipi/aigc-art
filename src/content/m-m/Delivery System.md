##### Construction of Micromotors via Emulsification Method

###### Yeast Gel Preparation

The yeast gel encapsulation was performed using an emulsification/internal gelation method. *Saccharomyces boulardii* CNCM Ⅰ-745 was first cultured overnight at 30 °C and 250 rpm in 50 mL of YPD medium in an Erlenmeyer flask. The culture was then diluted to an OD₆₀₀ of 1.2, washed with deionized water, and centrifuged at 1100 g to remove residual medium.

For encapsulation, 4.5 g of sodium alginate (Energy Chemical, China) was dissolved in 300 mL of deionized water in a 500 mL blue-cap bottle, shaken thoroughly, and labeled as “1.5% (w/v) SG-300.” The solution was sterilized at 121 °C for 15 min using an autoclave. Subsequently, 1 mL of yeast suspension was mixed with 10 mL of the 1.5% sodium alginate solution, followed by the addition of 0.1 g of nano-sized calcium carbonate (MREDA, China). The mixture was stirred continuously at 310 rpm.

An emulsion was then prepared by mixing the aqueous phase with 55 mL of liquid paraffin (MREDA, China) containing 0.5% Span 80® (Konoscience, China) at an oil-to-water phase ratio of 5:1. After 15 min of emulsification, 1.38 mL of glacial acetic acid (TGREAG, China) was added dropwise over 30 min to initiate internal gelation. The mixture was stirred for an additional 60 min to form stable yeast-loaded hydrogel microspheres. The resulting microspheres were washed with an acetate buffer (pH 5.5) prepared from acetic acid and anhydrous sodium acetate (MREDA, China) to remove residual oil.

###### pH Stability Test

For each tube, 1 mL of buffer solution was added as follows: tubes 1–3 received phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4; MACKLIN, China), tubes 4–6 received acetate buffer (pH 5.0) prepared using anhydrous sodium acetate (MREDA, China) and glacial acetic acid (TGREAG, China), and tubes 7–9 received 1 M hydrochloric acid solution (pH 1.0).

##### Construction of Micromotors via Microfluidic Method

###### System Construction and Material Preparation
A PDMS-glass hybrid microfluidic chip was fabricated using soft lithography to construct a platform for targeted gastric drug delivery. The process began with the preparation of an SU-8 patterned mold on a silicon wafer. In collaboration with DxFluidics, two microfluidic chips with five inlets were produced, specifically designed for generating Janus microspheres. The chip has a thickness of 3 mm, inlet holes of 0.5 mm, and is bonded to a 1 mm glass substrate.

###### Reagent Preparation and Microfluidic Configuration for Yeast Encapsulation
Analytical balance was used to precisely weigh the chemicals for the engineered yeast encapsulation system. Sodium alginate solution (2% w/w) was prepared by dissolving 6.00g of sodium alginate (Energy Chemical, China) in 300mL of deionized water within a 500mL blue-cap bottle, followed by stirring dissolution in an 80°C water bath for 30 minutes. After thorough dissolution, the suspension was sterilized via autoclaving at 121°C for 15 minutes. The solution was then mixed with *Saccharomyces boulardii* CNCM Ⅰ-745 at a concentration of 10⁸ cells/mL to create the yeast-alginate suspension. Simultaneously, 10% sodium carbonate solution and 1% calcium chloride solution were prepared using anhydrous sodium carbonate (TGREAG, China) and anhydrous calcium chloride (XILONG SCIENTIFIC, China). ##### Gelation, Collection, and Functional Validation of Gastric Delivery Microspheres
A 1 M HCl solution was prepared by diluting concentrated hydrochloric acid (6 M HCl, HUSHI, China) with deionized water. A 1 M hydrochloric acid solution was used to simulate the gastric acid environment. Gel-based micromotors were employed as the test samples. In a typical experiment, 400 mL of the 1 M HCl solution was added to a 1000 mL beaker to serve as the reaction medium. The hydrogel micromotors were then introduced into the solution, and their motion behavior was recorded using a digital camera. The propulsion and trajectory of the micromotors were subsequently analyzed by tracking their movement from the recorded videos.
